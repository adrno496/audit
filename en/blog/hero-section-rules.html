Hero section: 8 rules to convert from the first second

Above the fold, you play 80% of your conversion. Here are 8 timeless rules, illustrated with instructive counter-examples.

May 5, 20261,000 words

"Above the fold" refers to the area visible without scrolling. About 800 px tall on desktop, 600 px on mobile. It's your only chance to capture a visitor before they leave. Here are the eight rules.

Rule 1 — Answer "What is it and for whom?" in 5 seconds

The naive visitor test: show the page for 5 seconds to someone who doesn't know your product, hide the screen, ask: "What does it do, and for whom?". If they hesitate, your hero failed.

The headline must name the outcome you deliver to a precise audience. Avoid empty abstractions ("productivity", "digital transformation", "operational excellence").

Good: "Schedule 30 days of Instagram posts in 10 minutes. For solo community managers."
Bad: "The next-generation platform for community managers."

Rule 2 — The sub-headline clarifies, doesn't repeat

The sub-headline (the line right below the headline) serves to specify the mechanism or secondary benefit. It must not paraphrase the headline.

If you can delete your sub without losing info, it's redundant. Rewrite.

Rule 3 — One primary CTA, visible without scrolling

Colored button, large size, action and benefit-oriented text. Not "Submit", not "Learn more" — something like "Start my free trial" or "Audit my site".

If you have multiple personas (free / paid, B2B / B2C…), you can add a visually quieter secondary CTA (ghost button, link). But only one primary.

Rule 4 — The visual reinforces, doesn't distract

Options:

  • Product mockup: shows the product in action, ideal for SaaS
  • Real photo of target user: emotional, ideal for services and training
  • (Muted) video: 15-30s autoplay demo without sound, ideal for visual products
  • Custom illustration: differentiating but expensive, beware of generic "blob illustrations"

Avoid: generic stock photo of diverse smiling team in front of a MacBook. Everyone uses it, so no one remembers it.

Rule 5 — Immediate trust strip

Right under the hero or within it, add a mini-strip: customer logos, average rating, user count.

"Used by 12,000 freelancers · 4.8/5 on Trustpilot"

This reassurance line, dosed right, converts better than the best testimonials lower in the page. It's social proof applied to residual attention.

Rule 6 — No more than 3 competing visual elements

A hero containing: headline + sub + 2 CTAs + image + testimonial + customer logos + a promo banner + a countdown… is too much. The visitor's eye doesn't know where to look.

Rule of thumb: max 3 primary visual elements. Everything else is secondary (smaller size, less contrast) or goes lower in the page.

Rule 7 — Mobile-first, always

Over 60% of web traffic is mobile. Yet many pages are designed in Figma desktop, then "adapted" to mobile by proportional scaling.

Design for mobile first. If it works at 375 px wide with a thumb, it'll work on desktop. The reverse isn't true.

On mobile, the hero must fit in one scroll: headline + sub + CTA + a discreet visual. No customer logos above the fold on mobile (info overload).

Rule 8 — Test and iterate

The hero is the priority element to A/B test. It's the most impactful lever.

To test:

  • Headline (highest impact)
  • CTA wording
  • Visual type (mockup vs photo vs video)
  • Trust strip presence

One variable per test. Accept holding tests for 2-4 weeks for statistically solid signal.

Recap

  1. Answer "what & for whom" in 5s
  2. Sub clarifies, doesn't repeat
  3. One primary CTA visible without scrolling
  4. Visual that reinforces, not distracts
  5. Immediate trust strip
  6. Max 3 competing visual elements
  7. Mobile-first, always
  8. Test and iterate
