CTAs that convert: the complete guide (wording, color, placement)

"Learn more" is a dead CTA. Here's how to write, design and place an action button that converts — with the patterns that work and those that are obsolete.

The anatomy of a converting CTA

A high-performing CTA combines four layers:

  1. Wording (button text) — most impactful
  2. Color and contrast — visibility, differentiation
  3. Size and shape — click comfort, hierarchy
  4. Surrounding micro-copy — reassurance, objection handling

All four matter. Best wording in the world on an invisible button = useless.

Wording: 5 proven rules

1. First-person action verb

"Start my free trial" converts better than "Start a free trial". The "my" creates a sense of ownership.

Classic Unbounce study (2014): +90% conversion switching from "your" to "my". The exact result is imperfectly reproducible but the trend is solid.

2. Benefit or outcome, not action

Compare:

  • "Submit" — neutral action, no perceived benefit
  • "Get my quote" — clear benefit
  • "Get my quote in 2 minutes" — benefit + time reassurance

3. Avoid dead CTAs

  • "Learn more" (alternative: "See how it works")
  • "Click here" (alternative: name the action)
  • "Submit" (alternative: "Send my request")
  • "OK" (except on confirmation modals)

4. Address an explicit friction

If the visitor has a silent objection ("must be paid", "I'll have to give my card", "this'll take time"), address it in the button or just below.

"Start my free trial · no credit card"
"Audit my site · free, 30 seconds"

5. Stay short (2-5 words on the button max)

Beyond 5 words, the button becomes a mini-paragraph. Use micro-copy below to complete (see §6).

Color: what really matters

The myth "red converts better than green" is false. What matters is contrast against the rest of the page.

The button must be the most visible thing in its zone. If your page is dominated by blues, a blue button blends in — use orange, red, vivid green, whatever hue contrasts.

Practical rule: convert your page to grayscale (CSS filter: grayscale(1)). The button must clearly stand out monochrome — otherwise insufficient contrast.

Size, shape, spacing

Minimum recommended height: 44 px (Apple HIG mobile tap recommendation). Bigger is better, up to a point — an 80 px button feels aggressive.

Shape: rectangle with light rounded corners (8-12 px border-radius). Fully rounded "pill" buttons are trendy but slightly hurt readability.

Spacing: leave air around. A button cramped against text or other elements feels less clickable. 24-40 px above and below is a good start.

Placement: where and how many?

How many CTAs?

For most landing pages: 2 to 4 identical primary CTAs, spaced through the page. First in the hero, one in the pricing section, one at the bottom. No more.

The more different CTAs you add ("get started", "watch demo", "contact"), the more the visitor freezes. HubSpot studies show negative correlation between CTA count and conversion past 4-5 on a single page.

Where exactly?

  • Above the fold: mandatory. Visitors shouldn't have to scroll to click.
  • After each major argument: just presented a key benefit? Put a CTA right after.
  • Before the FAQ: those scrolling to the FAQ are in final-doubt phase.
  • Bottom of page: catches those who read everything.

Micro-copy: what surrounds the button

The micro-copy just below the button removes objections in 1 line:

  • "No credit card required" → removes payment objection
  • "Cancel anytime" → removes commitment perception
  • "14-day money-back guarantee" → removes risk
  • "No installation required" → removes technical friction

This line, in gray or quieter than the button, regularly bumps click-through rate by 10-20% in A/B tests.

On mobile: specific rules

  • Full-width: on mobile, make buttons span available width (with 16-24 px margin). Easier to tap.
  • Thumb position: a button at the bottom is naturally accessible. A top button requires reaching.
  • Sticky CTA: a "Audit my site" button fixed at the bottom of the screen on mobile can lift conversion 15-30% on long pages. Don't overuse — it's intrusive.

How to test a CTA

If you can only test one thing, test wording. It's the fastest lever to change with the most predictable impact.

Minimum methodology:

  1. Set up a 50/50 A/B test (Google Optimize is dead, but alternatives like PostHog, GrowthBook or Optimizely work)
  2. Hold the test 2-4 weeks minimum, or until 1,000 conversions on the losing variant
  3. Check statistical significance (at least 95% confidence)
  4. Log everything in an experiments journal to avoid re-testing the same thing twice

Recap

  • First-person action verb ("my")
  • Benefit or outcome, not action
  • Strong contrast against the rest of the page
  • 2-4 identical primary CTAs on the page
  • Micro-copy below to remove objections
  • Mobile: full-width, thumb-accessible
  • Test wording first
